It really depends on your strategy and objectives, but many quants tend to prefer  **more alphas with fewer points each**  because it promotes diversification, reduces single-alpha risk, and improves overall stability. Smaller, independent signals can combine to produce more robust performance.

That said,  **fewer alphas with more points**  can work well if the alphas are highly robust, well-validated, and stable across regimes.

In practice, a balanced approach often works best: prioritize  **quality first** , then diversify where possible.

### 评论 #10 (作者: SP39437, 时间: 6个月前)

The optimal allocation strategy really depends on your overall objectives, but many quants prefer distributing points across more alphas with smaller allocations each. This approach promotes diversification, reduces single-alpha risk, and generally improves portfolio stability. Independent, smaller signals can combine to produce robust overall performance without being overly sensitive to regime shifts.

Conversely, allocating more points to fewer alphas can work if those alphas are exceptionally robust, validated, and stable across market conditions. In practice, a balanced approach is usually most effective: prioritize high-quality alphas first, then diversify where possible to spread risk.

Adding too many signals indiscriminately can dilute your true edge, increase correlations, introduce noise, and make the portfolio harder to manage. Allocating points proportionally to confidence in an alpha’s out-of-sample performance—more points for strong, reliable signals, moderate points for average ones—is a practical way to optimize contributions.

How do you decide the threshold for point allocation when balancing high-conviction versus moderately performing alphas?
