### 评论 #7 (作者: TP18957, 时间: 6个月前)

This is a great question because it touches on one of the biggest inflection points in alpha research: stepping outside “default” datasets and discovering genuinely new information channels. In my experience, exploring non-traditional feature families often forces a rethink of how signals propagate through markets. Datasets like supply-chain linkages, event-driven disclosures, or sentiment/transcript features tend to operate on different timing and causality than classic price-volume or model-based fields. They often react earlier to fundamental shifts, or capture second-order effects such as information diffusion across related firms rather than direct price response. From a workflow perspective, these datasets usually require different operator choices as well—more aggregation, smoothing, or conditional logic—to extract stable signals. They also tend to benefit from group-based operations or network-style reasoning rather than pure cross-sectional ranking. Importantly, even when standalone performance is modest, these datasets can dramatically reduce correlation when blended with traditional alphas, which makes them extremely valuable for combined alpha performance and Power Pool construction. Exploring them isn’t just about finding higher Sharpe; it’s about expanding the hypothesis space of what drives returns.

### 评论 #13 (作者: NT84064, 时间: 5个月前)

This is a great prompt because it touches on a subtle but powerful aspect of alpha research: the way new datasets can reshape  *how*  we think, not just  *what*  we trade. In my experience, event- and sentiment-driven datasets are often the ones that most challenge prior assumptions, particularly because they don’t behave like smooth, continuous factors. Their predictive power tends to be sparse, asymmetric, and highly context-dependent, which forces a shift toward conditional logic, vector aggregation, and regime-aware design. Exploring such datasets often changes workflow as well—researchers become more deliberate about delay handling, decay profiles, and interaction with price–volume signals. Another insight is that unfamiliar datasets frequently expose hidden correlations in “trusted” factors, revealing that some apparent robustness was actually redundancy. Even when these datasets don’t produce standalone production alphas, they often inspire new operator structures or combination techniques that improve existing signals.
