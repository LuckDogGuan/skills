**What is short selling?**

“Going long” on a stock simply means buying it.  If the stock’s price rises after you buy it, you make money. If the price falls, you lose money. “Selling short” (a.k.a. shorting, going short) creates the inverse price-profit relationship, and is done as follows:

**Understanding the process**

When you short sell a stock, your  [broker](http://www.investopedia.com/terms/b/broker.asp)  lends it to you. The stock comes from the brokerage's own inventory, from another one of the firm's customers, or from another brokerage firm. The shares are sold and the proceeds are credited to your account. Sooner or later, you must "close" the short by buying back the same number of shares (called  [covering](http://www.investopedia.com/terms/s/shortcovering.asp) ) and returning them to your broker. If the share price drops, you can buy back the stock at the lower price and make a profit on the difference. If the price of the stock rises, you have to buy it back at the higher price, and you lose money.

**Duration and Costs**

Most of the time, you can hold a short for as long as you want, although interest is charged on margin accounts, so keeping a short sale open for a long time will cost more. However, you can be forced to cover if the lender wants the stock you borrowed back. Brokerages can't sell what they don't have, so you will either have to come up with new shares to borrow, or you'll have to cover. This is known as being " [called away"](http://www.investopedia.com/terms/c/calledaway.asp) . It doesn't happen often, but is possible if many investors are short selling a particular security.

**Obligations**

Because you don't own the stock you're short selling (you borrowed and then sold it), you must pay the lender of the stock any  [dividends](http://www.investopedia.com/terms/d/dividend.asp)  or  [rights](http://www.investopedia.com/terms/r/right.asp)  declared during the course of the loan. For more information, visit  [Investopedia - Short Selling](http://www.investopedia.com/university/shortselling/) .

Short selling is a strategy where an investor borrows shares of a stock and sells them in the hope that the price will fall. If the stock price decreases, the investor can buy it back at the lower price, return the shares, and keep the difference as profit. The costs of holding a short position include interest charges on margin accounts and potential obligations to cover the position if the broker demands the shares back.
