Hi, great question! The weight factor on your leaderboard reflects how much your contributions (mainly Alpha submissions) have influenced the overall WorldQuant Alpha ecosystem recently. When your weight factor is greater than zero, it means your Alphas are actively used in combinations and are adding marginal value. If it’s been zero for a while, it could mean that your recent submissions are either too similar to existing Alphas (high correlation or overlap), not diversified enough, or not improving the combined performance metrics. To improve it, try focusing on building orthogonal Alphas with low  `self_correlation` , explore new universes, less-used operators, and apply proper neutralization. Also, make sure to follow the Gen2 guidelines, as the system now rewards uniqueness and marginal impact more than ever. Keep iterating, track your  `author_prod_correlation` , and experiment with lower turnover or new styles. Hope this helps clarify it — don’t give up!

### 评论 #3 (作者: TP18957, 时间: 1年前)

The  **weight factor**  reflects the capital allocation WorldQuant assigns to your selected alpha signals within their production models. In other words, it's a measure of how much your alpha is being trusted and used in live trading environments. A weight of  **zero**  means none of your alphas are currently used with capital backing, which is common for new consultants. As your alphas get selected and show stable, diverse, and valuable performance over time, your weight factor can gradually increase. This process usually takes  **6–12 months** , especially if you're submitting regularly and optimizing your signals. Participating in Power Pool can also increase visibility and usage probability.

### 评论 #7 (作者: SK14400, 时间: 1年前)

The  **weight factor**  on your  **WorldQuant Alpha leaderboard**  indicates how much  **influence your alpha has in the firm's internal strategy mix** . If the weight is  **zero** , it means that currently, your alpha is  **not being used in production**  or in any live model. This can happen even if your alpha performs well, due to overlapping signals, redundancy, or risk constraints.

To increase your alpha’s weight:

- Ensure it is  **orthogonal**  (not highly correlated) to existing production alphas.
- Aim for  **high Sharpe**  and  **low turnover** .
- Run  **subuniverse Sharpe tests**  to verify robustness.

So in essence, a weight of zero doesn’t mean the alpha is bad—it just isn’t adding  **unique value**  to the current portfolio mix.
