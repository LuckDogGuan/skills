# When Combining Alphas Actually Makes Things Worse

## 帖子正文

We’re often told to diversify our alphas, but combining signals can sometimes reduce performance instead of improving it. More alphas does not automatically mean a better portfolio. The key issue is correlation. Two signals may look different in formula but be economically identical ;for example, a value factor and an earnings-yield variation often load on the same underlying exposure. In that case, combining them doesn’t increase breadth or improve Sharpe; it simply doubles the same risk. That’s why checking cross-sectional correlation, PnL correlation, and factor overlap is essential.There’s also the problem of false diversification. Mixing momentum with reversal or quality with low volatility may appear diversified, but in certain regimes they can fail together. True diversification comes from different economic drivers, different horizons, and different reaction speeds  not just different expressions. Weighting matters too. Adding weaker signals can dilute a strong alpha, and portfolio IC is not the average of individual ICs. In fact, Strong Alpha + Weak Alpha can be worse than Strong Alpha alone.Finally, signal compatibility is often ignored. Combining high-turnover and low-turnover alphas can increase costs and destabilize rebalancing. The goal is orthogonality, not quantity. If new alphas don’t add independent breadth, you’re just stacking the same exposure in disguise. In alpha construction, quality of diversification beats quantity of formulas.

### 评论 #2 (作者: DO92149, 时间: 1个月前)

In addition, Alpha diversification is not about maximizing the number of formulas ; it’s about maximizing independent sources of edge. Two alphas with different expressions can still share the same economic exposure, meaning their returns become highly correlated during stress regimes. Without measuring correlation structure, factor overlap, and turnover compatibility, “diversification” can become hidden concentration. True portfolio improvement comes from orthogonal signals driven by different mechanisms, horizons, and market reactions. In practice, adding a weak or redundant alpha often dilutes portfolio IC and increases implementation costs rather than improving robustness. The objective is not more signals, but more independent breadth.
