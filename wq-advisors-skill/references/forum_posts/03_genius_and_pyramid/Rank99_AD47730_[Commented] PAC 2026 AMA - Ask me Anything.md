### 评论 #38 (作者: ZC81788, 时间: 4天前)

Building on the OP's question — I have a EUR/SECTOR/TOP2500/delay=1 alpha that can serve as a reference point. Its IS PnL is 11.72M vs investabilityConstrained PnL of 10.35M, a gap of roughly 1.37M; IS Sharpe 4.32 vs investabilityConstrained Sharpe 3.91. While this isn't a direct FastExpr↔Python comparison, pasteurization under investabilityConstrained sets non-tradable instruments to NaN, which shifts cross-sectional weights — the magnitude here is consistent with typical differences seen after pasteurization weight adjustments.YW42946's three directions are largely correct. Adding one specific mechanism: FastExpr's internal tie-breaking in rank() — the ordering of instruments with identical values — may differ from Python's numpy.argsort(kind='quicksort') default behavior, since quicksort is unstable and doesn't guarantee relative order for equal elements. If an alpha signal frequently produces identical values across certain instruments, this creates cross-sectional weight allocation differences that affect daily PnL, even when the overall PnL series maintains a high Pearson correlation (like the 0.95+ the OP observed). When porting to Python, try using mergesort (a stable sorting option in numpy) explicitly for ts_rank or rank operations, and compare PnL series under pasteurization=ON vs OFF on a day-by-day basis to identify dates with dense ties.
