## 帖子正文

Abstract:

Human capital is a key factor in value creation in the modern corporation. Yet the disclosure of investment in human capital is scant. We propose that a company’s online job postings are disclosures made outside of the investor relations channel that contain forward-looking information that could be informative to investors about future growth. We find that changes in the number of job postings are positively associated with changes in future performance and that this relation is stronger when postings likely represent growth rather than replacement. Consistent with job postings providing new information to the market, investors react positively to changes in the number of job postings. The market reaction to postings is stronger when firms are likely to be hiring for growth rather than replacement and for firms with low labor intensity (and therefore high marginal productivity of labor).

Author: Elizabeth Gutiérrez, Ben Lourie, Alex Nekrasov , and Terry Shevlin

Year: 2020

### 评论 #1 (作者: DK20528, 时间: 1年前)

This paper offers a fascinating perspective on the intersection of labor market signals and financial decision-making. The authors explore whether information embedded in firms' online job postings can serve as predictive indicators for firm performance and investor behavior. This novel approach enriches the literature by introducing an unconventional yet intuitive source of data that links labor demand with capital market outcomes.

The use of job postings as a forward-looking indicator is particularly insightful because they reflect firms' expectations about future growth, strategic initiatives, and economic conditions. The paper's empirical results demonstrating the relationship between job postings and stock returns highlight the potential for these postings to serve as an alternative data source for investors. Moreover, the discussion about the types of job postings that are most informative, such as those related to R&D or strategic positions, adds a layer of granularity that is highly relevant for practitioners.

However, the study could benefit from further examination in a few areas:

1. **Sectoral Differences:**  Job posting activity and its informativeness likely vary across industries. A deeper exploration of sector-specific trends could improve the applicability of the findings.
2. **Time Sensitivity:**  The lag between job posting activity and its observable effects on firm performance might differ depending on the nature of the role. Investigating this time dimension could add precision to the analysis.
3. **Macroeconomic Conditions:**  The informativeness of job postings may change during economic downturns or booms. Including this dimension could enhance the robustness of the findings.

### Questions Regarding the Paper:

1. **Job Posting Metrics:**  What specific aspects of job postings (e.g., volume, type of roles, geographic location) are most predictive of firm performance? Did the authors employ any natural language processing (NLP) techniques to analyze the content of the postings?
2. **Causality and Confounding Factors:**  How does the study address potential confounding factors, such as firms engaging in high levels of job postings simply reflecting broader market trends or industry-specific growth?
3. **Market Reaction:**  Does the paper provide insights into whether and how quickly investors incorporate job posting information into stock prices? Are there differences in how retail and institutional investors react to such information?
4. **Data Sources and Coverage:**  What data sources were used for collecting online job postings, and how comprehensive was the dataset? Were there any limitations in coverage, such as missing postings from smaller firms or firms operating in less tech-savvy industries?
5. **Behavioral Implications:**  Does the paper discuss how the informativeness of job postings could potentially create inefficiencies or opportunities in the market due to investors’ varying ability to process this information?
6. **Long-term Implications:**  To what extent do job postings predict sustained firm performance versus short-term market reactions?

Would you like further help analyzing or critiquing specific findings from the paper?
