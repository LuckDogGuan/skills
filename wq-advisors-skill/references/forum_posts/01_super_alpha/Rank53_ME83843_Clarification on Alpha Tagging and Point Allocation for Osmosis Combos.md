### 评论 #11 (作者: NT84064, 时间: 6个月前)

For Osmosis combos, tagging and point allocation are both more influential than they first appear, because they directly affect how your submission is interpreted and combined. Tagging should reflect the  *economic intent*  and dominant driver of each alpha rather than the surface expression — for example, a signal using price operators but driven by liquidity imbalance should be tagged accordingly, not generically as price. This helps avoid unintended clustering and improves diversification at the system level. Regarding points, allocation is explicitly assigned per alpha within the combo, not inferred implicitly, so it’s worth thinking in portfolio terms. Many experienced participants start with higher weights on a small core of robust, low-correlation alphas, then allocate smaller points to more experimental or higher-turnover ideas. Confidence, uniqueness, and transaction costs should all factor in, but stability and correlation usually dominate final decisions. Iterating weights based on combined behavior, not standalone Sharpe, is often the most effective approach.

### 评论 #21 (作者: NT84064, 时间: 5个月前)

This is a very relevant operational question, especially for participants preparing Osmosis combos on  **WorldQuant Brain** , where execution details can materially affect outcomes. From a practical standpoint, alpha tagging should reflect the  **primary economic driver**  of each alpha, not every secondary transformation it contains. Clean tagging helps ensure that the combo optimizer does not overweight highly correlated signals disguised under different labels. Over-tagging often leads to redundancy at the pool level, while under-tagging can misrepresent risk exposures.

Regarding point or weight allocation, experienced participants typically treat it as an  **expression of conviction and diversification** , rather than a direct function of standalone Sharpe. Alphas with stable IC, low turnover, and low correlation to the rest of the combo tend to deserve higher allocation, even if their individual Sharpe is modest. Transaction costs and decay should also influence weighting; fast, noisy signals rarely justify heavy allocation. In most cases, point allocation is explicit per alpha within the combo, but it should be informed by combo-level diagnostics such as marginal contribution to Sharpe and drawdown behavior. The key is to think in portfolio terms: points are not rewards for performance, but tools to balance robustness and diversification.
