### 评论 #5 (作者: VM20865, 时间: 8个月前)

Yes, using alphas with a low number of fields is often a superior strategy for building a high-performing combination alpha. Simple, specialized alphas contribute more effectively to a robust and diversified portfolio.

A collection of simple, specialized alphas is more likely to be uncorrelated. Each one captures a different piece of the market puzzle. Combining them reduces overall portfolio volatility and improves your Sharpe ratio.

Low-field alphas are more constrained. Their simplicity forces them to rely on more fundamental and persistent market dynamics. This makes them more robust and stable over time.

When a simple alpha stops working, it is easier to understand why. Diagnosing a failing alpha with a dozen inputs and complex transformations is difficult.

### 评论 #6 (作者: UN28170, 时间: 8个月前)

That’s a great question — and yes,  **the number of fields per alpha**  (i.e., how many data features or transformations a single formula uses) can absolutely influence  **combo alpha performance** , though not in a purely linear way.

In general,  **low-field alphas**  — those that rely on fewer input fields — tend to be:

1. **More interpretable**  and easier to analyze,
2. **Less prone to overfitting** , and
3. **Potentially more orthogonal**  when combined with other simple alphas.

This simplicity often makes them valuable  **building blocks**  for a strong combo alpha. When multiple low-field signals are combined, each contributes a small but distinct perspective on market behavior, which can  **reduce correlation**  and  **stabilize the aggregate performance** .

However, there’s a balance to strike:

- If an alpha has  *too few fields*  or an overly simple structure, it may carry  **weak standalone predictive power**  (low Sharpe or low Fitness).
- But if your combo uses  **many diverse low-field alphas**  that each capture slightly different phenomena (momentum, mean reversion, volatility, liquidity, etc.), the  **ensemble’s performance can improve significantly** , as noise cancels out and true signals reinforce each other.

In contrast,  **high-field alphas**  can sometimes overlap in information or behave similarly, leading to redundancy when combined — even if they have good individual Sharpe ratios.

So, to summarize:

> **Low-field alphas, when diverse and well-designed, often serve as excellent ingredients for high-performing combos.**
> The key isn’t how simple each alpha is individually, but how  **uncorrelated and complementary**  they are collectively.
