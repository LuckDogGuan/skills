### 评论 #10 (作者: TP18957, 时间: 6个月前)

This is a good question to raise, especially as the India region is still relatively new and people are trying to understand which submission conventions really matter. From a technical and process perspective, the community feedback so far is consistent: alpha descriptions in the India region do not affect scoring, evaluation metrics, or PowerPool eligibility. The simulation, validation, and acceptance pipeline relies entirely on quantitative outputs and risk checks, not textual explanations. That said, descriptions still play an important  *workflow*  role. When you iterate on multiple variants of similar ideas—especially across regions—having a concise description helps you distinguish intent, dataset usage, and signal logic months later. This becomes even more valuable if you reuse the alpha as a child in SuperAlphas or revisit it after platform updates. In short, descriptions are not part of the evaluation engine, but they are part of good research hygiene and long-term alpha management.

### 评论 #12 (作者: NT84064, 时间: 5个月前)

This is a good question, and it touches on an important distinction between  *submission requirements*  and  *best research practice* . Even if the India region currently allows alpha submissions without a description, providing one still has indirect but meaningful value. While descriptions may not explicitly factor into scoring metrics like Sharpe, Fitness, or Turnover, they help clarify the economic intuition and structural intent behind the signal. This becomes especially relevant when reviewing performance degradation, diagnosing correlation issues, or revisiting older alphas after regime shifts. A clear description also forces discipline during development—it helps ensure the alpha is hypothesis-driven rather than purely combinatorial. In regions like India, where market structure and data behavior may evolve rapidly, having documented intent makes it easier to adapt or extend successful ideas. Additionally, if the alpha is ever reviewed, discussed, or reused in portfolio construction contexts, a description improves interpretability and reduces the risk of misclassification or redundant research.
