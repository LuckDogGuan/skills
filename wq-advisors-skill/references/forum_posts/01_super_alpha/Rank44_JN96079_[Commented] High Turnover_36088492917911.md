So in your case, if your alpha has top turnover, then  **a return of more than 20%**  should be your target when creating alphas. Also, you should target a high margin if you have the same high turnover levels to cater to the trading costs!

High turnover can indicate strong responsiveness but also leads to higher transaction costs and market impact. To justify such costs, the alpha’s expected returns should significantly exceed those of lower-turnover strategies. A turnover-adjusted performance measure, such as the information ratio divided by √turnover, can help assess efficiency. In practice, maintaining turnover within the 10–25% range ensures a balanced trade-off between adaptability, execution efficiency, and long-term alpha stability.

### 评论 #6 (作者: AY28568, 时间: 7个月前)

High turnover often indicates frequent trading and faster signal decay, which can boost gross returns but also increase transaction costs and slippage. The ideal return target depends on your trading frequency, liquidity, and cost structure. For a high-turnover alpha, you might aim for higher gross returns (e.g., 20–40% annually) to offset costs. However, it’s wise to keep turnover within a sustainable range typically between 200% and 600% annually  balancing alpha decay and cost efficiency. Ultimately, optimizing turnover should focus on maximizing net returns after accounting for all trading expenses.
