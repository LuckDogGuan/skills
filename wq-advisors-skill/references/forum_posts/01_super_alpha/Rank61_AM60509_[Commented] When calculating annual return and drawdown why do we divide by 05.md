### **Margin Formula: Margin = PnL / Total Dollars Traded**

When you see the formula  **Margin = PnL / Total Dollars Traded** , it might seem confusing at first. Let's break it down:

- **PnL** : This is the  **Profit and Loss**  generated by the trades over a specific period.
- **Total Dollars Traded** : This refers to the  **total value of the trades**  conducted, which is the sum of all the trades made. This is often the gross value of the positions (i.e., not just the net capital at risk but the total amount traded, including both sides of any leveraged positions).

#### Why It Could Seem Confusing:

You mentioned a return of 11% and a margin of 40%. These numbers don’t seem to match intuitively at first. Let's see how margin and return relate:

- **Return** : The return usually refers to the  **net profit or loss**  relative to the initial capital or net worth.
- **Margin** : In the context of the formula  **Margin = PnL / Total Dollars Traded** , margin refers to the  **ratio of profit**  relative to the total amount of money traded (including both sides of the trade, i.e., the gross capital involved, not just the net capital at risk).

Conclusion:

- The assumption of only half of the capital being "active" is often related to how much of your total capital is actually being deployed in trades (not just sitting in cash or used for margin).
- The  **margin formula**  shows the  **ratio of profit relative to the total capital traded**  (gross notional value), which can be higher than the actual return on your invested capital, especially when leverage is involved.

### 评论 #15 (作者: WX16829, 时间: 1年前)

The assumption that "half of the capital would be active on average" is often used in simplified models to account for the fact that not all capital is always fully invested. Here are a few reasons why this assumption might be used:

#### **Reasons for the Assumption:**

1. **Cash Reserves and Liquidity:**
   - In real-world trading, traders and investors often keep a portion of their capital in cash or highly liquid instruments (like T-bills) to cover unexpected expenses, margin calls, or to take advantage of new investment opportunities. This means that not all capital is actively invested at any given time.
2. **Surplus and Reinvestment:**
   - If you generate a surplus (profit) from your investments, you might not immediately reinvest it. Instead, you might hold it in cash or other low-risk assets until you find a suitable new investment. This means that the surplus is not contributing to the active capital generating returns.
3. **Risk Management:**
   - Traders often use stop-losses or take profits at certain levels, which means they might not be fully invested at all times. Additionally, some strategies involve holding a portion of capital in reserve to manage risk.
