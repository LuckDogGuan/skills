## 帖子正文

The  **Japan (JPN) Region**  universe includes roughly  **1,200–1,600 stocks** , making it the largest ASI single-country market by instrument count and one of the world’s most liquid and important markets. Although JPN Alphas resemble ASI-style signals, they also require Japan-specific thinking and data.

For  **HKG, KOR, and TWN**  regions,  **Max Trade must be enabled**  when simulating or submitting Alphas.

**Key characteristics of the Japanese market:**

- Lower expected Alpha returns compared to other regions.
- Weak momentum; stronger  **seasonality** , leading to lower Sharpe ratios.

**Good starting points for Alpha ideas:**

- **Value-style strategies** , especially those captured in models like  *model77* .
- **Price-volume Alphas** , which tend to perform well.

### 评论 #5 (作者: NT84064, 时间: 5个月前)

This post captures several important structural realities of the JPN universe that researchers often underestimate when applying generic ASI logic. The large instrument count combined with very high liquidity fundamentally changes the signal-to-noise profile: cross-sectional effects are more diluted, and aggressive momentum assumptions tend to break down. The observation about weak momentum and stronger seasonality is especially critical, as many global templates implicitly rely on medium-term trend persistence that simply does not materialize as reliably in Japan. This directly explains the lower achievable Sharpe ratios and requires tighter turnover control and longer holding horizons. Emphasizing value-style signals, such as those embedded in model77, makes sense given Japan’s historical valuation dispersion and accounting-driven effects. Price-volume alphas also benefit from the market’s depth and stability, allowing microstructure signals to survive trading costs. Finally, the operational reminder about enabling Max Trade for HKG, KOR, and TWN is practical and prevents misleading simulations when expanding research across ASI subregions.
